On Knowing Nothing in Particular

There is a specific kind of freedom in being bad at many things rather than good at one. The dilettante does not suffer the anxiety of the specialist. He is not haunted by the thought that somewhere, in some institution, a person more rigorous than himself is dismantling every position he has tentatively arrived at. He holds his conclusions lightly, because he arrived at them lightly, and lets them go the same way.

This is not, of course, how any serious person is supposed to operate. Seriousness demands depth, consistency, a bibliography. It demands that you have read the secondary literature, that you know which debates have been settled and which remain live, that you can defend a position under examination. The dilettante fails all of these tests spectacularly.

The fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing. I have always suspected the fox is having more fun.

And yet. There is something that the dilettante sees that the specialist sometimes cannot, which is the shape of the whole. Not its texture, not its grain, not its fine detail, but its general silhouette against whatever else surrounds it. Whether that matters depends entirely on what you are trying to do.

I am not trying to do anything in particular. That is, perhaps, the most honest thing I can say about this enterprise. But I just suppose we'll see how to go about things in the coming future.
