Is the Law Wordplay?

The question I've asked is high stakes: if it's just wordplay, we're making a big deal out of nothing and the law is, like beauty, in the beholder's eye. Attempts at rationalizing it are in vain. There's everything to lose for what we triumphantly herald as the institution of law.

There are two views: yes and no. Those who say yes are likely disillusioned with the law in practice. These are practical-minded folks, largely unconcerned with academic niceties. More power to them too - as lawyers who practice in courts, this should be our main concern too. There's a fair bit of academic support of this position too: legal realism. This camp maintains that the law is simply a prediction of what the courts will say. It's not some towering analytical discipline built on the edifice of logic and doctrine. That is a mere smokescreen for what hides beneath: an awry mess of thoughts that are directed towards answering 'what is the just decision' here? The second camp, those who say no, are often the academic kind. They claim the law is, roughly speaking, akin to a science. It is self-contained, closed body of doctrine. It is closed to subjectivity.

The law, analytically speaking, is not just some prediction of what courts, based on the relative equities of the case, will decide; no. The law is boundedly objective. The bounds, as Owen says, are 'disciplining rules' and 'an intellectual community'. I don't care for abstraction where concreteness will do. I was reading an article on Fiduciary law today. The question was: how do you define a fiduciary relationship? This is an important but embarrassingly unresolved question in private law. If you're a fiduciary, you're subject to fiduciary duties: good faith, prudence, no-conflict and no-profit. In more than 200 years of jurisprudence, the legal community is divided in doing something as relatively basic as defining a fiduciary. It's one of those things--I can't define it but I know it when I see it (believe it or not, this standard was judicially endorsed by some antidiluvean English case as the test for obscenity). Anyway, sophisticated jurists will rebuff this method, because the law performs an important societal function as well: to guide people. So what is a fiduciary? I'm not interested in answering this question here. But that we can't define a fiduciary speaks to the larger issue: is law just wordplay? If I just have to argue that person X is a fiduciary to person Y because he bears a relationship that puts Y at a position of vulnerability and exercises discretionary powers on behalf of Y, this definition is both overinclusive and underinclusive. So if no definition is really a workable definition, it's all just wordplay, correct?

Maybe. But there's value to taxonomizing the morass that is law. Take unjust enrichment. They called it quasi-contract, money had and received, enrichment without cause and whatever else. Peter Birks devoted his life to taxonomizing this mess. He picked it apart from first principles. Distinguished unjust enrichment from contract and tort. Forged its relationship with restitution. Clarified the state of the law. I think there's some beauty to this. Apart from the fact that the law turns on words, and words are vague and infinitely limited, the law has a social engineering angle. Even something like contract or unjust enrichment is decided on a basis that's comparable to deciding a case on discrimination under Art.14 under the Constitution. Judges in both cases are concerned with questions of justice and fairness. The law is a technical hindrance in their otherwise unfettered creativity and drive to deliver the optimally just result. Perhaps the law is wordplay. But what is wordplay anyway? Arguing about the meaning of words? Don't philosophers do that? Don't scientists do that? When we're wordplaying, the goal is a higher truth: a determination of what the word stands for. Wordplay is the entry point. What lies deeper is the terrain of concepts. Beneath concepts lies more abstract notions of justice and fairness. The pedigree of words is often misunderstood: they're the most concrete manifestation of something far more terrifying abstruse. Of course you're troubled by it and call it wordplay. But when you really try to decipher what 'fiduciary' means, you ask what importance that word services in the law. You then arrive at a concept: 'vulnerability' or 'discretionary power'. These are one level removed from fiduciary in terms of abstraction, but still very concrete as applied. But you take it a step deeper, and another, and another. You're now in terrifying territory. The ter ritory of guesses. The territory of subjectivity--the behemoth that torments philosophers, lawyers and anyone involved in the arts.

To answer the question: the law is wordplay. But the 'play' is actually a war.
